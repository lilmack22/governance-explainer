Band Aid solutions may provide important short run relief, but because they don't tackle the root governance failures behind these gaping gaps in basic services (education, healthcare, sanitation, food, etc), they fail to generating self-sustaining development. 

SHORT-RUN RELIEF vs LONG-RUN SUSTAINABILITY

SHORT-RUN (RELIEF / DELIVERY)
	•	services are provided
	•	outcomes improve while intervention is active
	•	external actors fill gaps

LONG-RUN (SELF-SUSTAINING SYSTEMS)
	•	institutions deliver services without external support
	•	incentives align with implementation
	•	capacity exists within the state/system
	•	outcomes persist after intervention ends

The central question is not only whether an intervention works, but whether it continues to work once external support is withdrawn.

That’s the heart of it.

⸻

HOW THIS CONNECTS TO THE “ROOT CAUSE” IDEA

Many development challenges persist not because solutions are unknown, but because the underlying political and institutional conditions that would sustain those solutions are not in place.

So:
	•	downstream → can deliver outcomes
	•	upstream → determines whether those outcomes persist

⸻

EVEN TIGHTER VERSION (MORE ANALYTICAL)

Many interventions successfully deliver services or improve outcomes in the short run. But when underlying incentives, institutions, and state capacity remain unchanged, these gains often depend on continued external support (which is typically variable -- think USAID being dismantled post Trump; funds getting redirected as new global crises emerge; etc) and not sufficient enough in scale and in legal/institutional power within country to fully address the depth of need. 

Accountable, high capacity governments, in contrast, can much better and more sustainable help people to meet a range of basic needs and more -- because capable democratic governments are structured by design to aggregate information about people's needs and to deliver services at scale to meet those needs. Getting those kinds of strong, accountable governments in place can therefore solve multiple needs at once. The core challenge is how to establish this kind of effective governance -- it's a tall order but a worthy endeavor nonetheless because of the immeasurable (and far more sustainable) social benefits that such a transformation could produce.

SYMPTOMS METAPHOR

	•	treating symptoms → patient improves
	•	but if underlying condition persists → relapse
	•	continued treatment required indefinitely

When underlying conditions are not addressed, improvement may require continuous intervention rather than leading to durable recovery.

A VERSION THAT FEELS VERY “YOU” (POLITICAL ECONOMY)

Many development interventions focus on delivering services or addressing immediate gaps. These can produce meaningful improvements. But whether those improvements persist depends on underlying political and institutional conditions — including incentives, state capacity, and accountability. When those conditions are weak, progress often relies on continued external support rather than becoming self-sustaining.
