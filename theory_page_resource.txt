WHAT CAUSES DEVELOPMENT?
“When political leaders expect to stay in power, they invest in long-term growth.”
→ Known as stationary bandit logic; associated with Mancur Olson. Rulers with long time horizons provide public goods and protect property.
“Economic growth is more likely when elites cannot easily extract wealth from others.”
→ Inclusive vs. extractive institutions framework; Daron Acemoglu & James A. Robinson.
“States that can tax, administer, and enforce rules are more capable of generating development.”
→ State capacity theory; Francis Fukuyama and historical institutionalists.
“Development happens when economies shift from low-productivity sectors (like subsistence agriculture) to higher-productivity ones.”
→ Structural transformation; Arthur Lewis dual-sector model.
“Countries grow when they solve the most binding constraint rather than trying to fix everything at once.”
→ Growth diagnostics; Dani Rodrik.
“Access to education, health, and basic capabilities expands people’s ability to participate in the economy.”
→ Capability approach; Amartya Sen.
“Strong coordination between the state and firms can accelerate industrialization.”
→ Developmental state model (East Asia).
“Natural resource wealth can weaken incentives to build productive institutions.”
→ Resource curse literature.
WHAT CAUSES DEMOCRATIZATION?
STRUCTURAL + LONG-RUN
“As living standards rise, people demand more political participation.”
→ Modernization theory; Seymour Martin Lipset.
“When inequality is high, elites resist democracy because they fear redistribution.”
→ Redistribution models; Acemoglu & Robinson.
ELITES, STRATEGY, AND REGIME DYNAMICS
“Leaders stay in power by rewarding just enough supporters to survive.”
→ Selectorate theory; Bruce Bueno de Mesquita. Focus on winning coalition size.
“When elites split into factions, openings for democratization emerge.”
→ Softliners vs. hardliners in authoritarian regimes; Guillermo O'Donnell & Philippe Schmitter.
“Democracy often emerges from negotiated bargains rather than mass overthrow.”
→ Pacted transitions; Southern Europe / Latin America transitions literature.
“Rulers may allow elections not to lose power, but to better manage elites and opposition.”
→ Electoral authoritarianism; Andreas Schedler.
“Some regimes maintain the appearance of democracy while systematically undermining competition.”
→ Competitive authoritarianism; Steven Levitsky & Lucan Way.
MASS POLITICS
“Organized groups (labor, civil society) can force elites to expand political rights.”
→ Social movement and labor-based democratization theories; Charles Tilly.
“Access to information makes it easier for citizens to hold leaders accountable.”
→ Media/accountability models.
SEQUENCING & STATE BUILDING
“Effective states usually emerge before stable democracies.”
→ State-first argument; Samuel P. Huntington and Francis Fukuyama.
CLIENTELISM & DISTRIBUTION
“Politicians maintain support by distributing targeted benefits rather than broad public goods.”
→ Clientelism; Susan C. Stokes and others.
“When voters depend on targeted benefits, programmatic politics and accountability are weaker.”
→ Clientelism vs. programmatic competition literature.
WHAT REDUCES CONFLICT & VIOLENCE?
STATE & GOVERNANCE
“Violence declines when the state can monopolize coercion and enforce rules.”
→ State capacity / Weberian state logic.
“People are less likely to support violence when they view the state as legitimate.”
→ Legitimacy frameworks (incl. ALC-type thinking).
ELITES & COMMITMENT PROBLEMS
“Conflicts persist when groups cannot credibly commit to future peace.”
→ Commitment problem; James Fearon.
“Power-sharing can reduce violence by reassuring losing groups.”
→ Power-sharing literature.
“Violence declines when elites reach stable political settlements over power and resources.”
→ Political settlements framework.
ECONOMIC INCENTIVES
“People are less likely to join armed groups when they have better economic alternatives.”
→ Opportunity cost models; Paul Collier.
“Conflict is more likely when groups face systematic economic or political inequalities.”
→ Horizontal inequalities; Frances Stewart.
NON-STATE ACTORS & HYBRID GOVERNANCE
“Governments sometimes tolerate or even rely on non-state actors to provide governance in areas they cannot or prefer not to control.”
→ Rebel governance / hybrid governance literature; Stathis Kalyvas, Ana Arjona.
“Non-state armed groups can reduce violence locally when they establish predictable rules and governance.”
→ Rebel governance / order-making in civil war.
“States may strategically outsource coercion or governance to militias to maintain plausible deniability.”
→ Paramilitarism / indirect rule literature.
MICRO-LEVEL DYNAMICS
“Civilians collaborate with armed actors when it increases their security relative to alternatives.”
→ Civil war microfoundations; Kalyvas.
“Selective, targeted violence is more effective for control than indiscriminate violence.”
→ Violence logic in civil war.
INTERNATIONAL & PEACEBUILDING
“External actors can reduce violence by monitoring and enforcing peace agreements.”
→ Peacekeeping effectiveness; United Nations.
“Disarming and reintegrating fighters reduces the risk of renewed violence.”
→ DDR frameworks.
CROSS-CUTTING 
“Political outcomes depend on how incentives are structured for key actors.”
→ Political economy / rationalist approaches.
“Many bad outcomes persist because actors cannot coordinate on better equilibria.”
→ Coordination failure frameworks.
“Institutions persist because they benefit powerful groups, even if they are inefficient.”
→ Historical institutionalism / path dependence.
